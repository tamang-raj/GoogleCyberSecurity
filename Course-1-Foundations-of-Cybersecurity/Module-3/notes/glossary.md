# Terms and Definitions – Course 1, Module 3

## Asset
An item perceived as having value to an organization.

**Examples:**
- Customer data
- Employee records
- Servers
- Software applications
- Intellectual property

---

## Availability
The principle that **data and systems are accessible to authorized users whenever needed**.

### Examples
- System backups
- Redundant servers
- Disaster recovery plans
- Load balancing

**CIA Triad Component:** Availability

---

## Compliance
The process of **adhering to internal organizational standards and external laws, regulations, and industry requirements**.

### Examples
- Following company security policies
- Meeting GDPR requirements
- Complying with HIPAA regulations

---

## Confidentiality
The principle that **only authorized users can access specific data or assets**.

### Examples
- Encryption
- Password protection
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Access control lists (ACLs)

**CIA Triad Component:** Confidentiality

---

## Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability (CIA) Triad
A foundational cybersecurity model that helps organizations manage risk and design secure systems.

### Components

### Confidentiality
Protects data from unauthorized access.

### Integrity
Ensures data is accurate, authentic, complete, and reliable.

### Availability
Ensures authorized users have timely access to data and systems.

---

## Hacktivist
A person or group that uses hacking techniques to promote political, social, or ideological causes.

### Common Activities
- Website defacement
- Data leaks
- Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks
- Public disclosure of confidential information

---

## Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)
A **United States federal law** that protects patients' health information and establishes standards for safeguarding medical records.

### HIPAA Protects
- Medical records
- Health insurance information
- Treatment history
- Patient billing information

---

## Integrity
The principle that **data remains correct, authentic, complete, and reliable throughout its lifecycle**.

### Examples
- Hashing
- Digital signatures
- File integrity monitoring

**CIA Triad Component:** Integrity

---

## National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)
A **voluntary cybersecurity framework** consisting of standards, guidelines, and best practices to help organizations manage cybersecurity risk.

### NIST CSF Core Functions

1. Govern
2. Identify
3. Protect
4. Detect
5. Respond
6. Recover

---

## Privacy Protection
The practice of **safeguarding personal information from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, or misuse**.

### Examples
- Restricting access to employee records
- Encrypting customer information
- Following data privacy laws

---

## Protected Health Information (PHI)
Information related to an individual's **past, present, or future physical or mental health** that is protected under HIPAA.

### Examples
- Medical records
- Laboratory results
- Health insurance information
- Prescription history
- Medical diagnoses

---

## Security Architecture
A structured security design composed of technologies, policies, procedures, and processes used to protect an organization's systems, networks, and data.

### Components
- Firewalls
- Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
- Encryption
- Identity and Access Management (IAM)
- Security policies

---

## Security Controls
Safeguards implemented to **reduce specific cybersecurity risks**.

### Types of Security Controls

#### Administrative Controls
- Security policies
- Employee training
- Procedures

#### Technical Controls
- Firewalls
- Antivirus software
- Encryption
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

#### Physical Controls
- Security cameras
- Locks
- Access badges
- Security guards

---

## Security Ethics
Professional guidelines that help cybersecurity professionals make responsible, lawful, and ethical decisions.

### Principles
- Follow laws and regulations
- Maintain confidentiality
- Protect privacy
- Act with integrity
- Follow organizational policies

---

## Security Frameworks
Structured guidelines that help organizations develop cybersecurity strategies to reduce risks and protect information.

### Common Frameworks
- NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)
- ISO/IEC 27001
- CIS Controls
- COBIT

### Benefits
- Improve security posture
- Reduce cybersecurity risks
- Support compliance
- Align security with business objectives

---

## Security Governance
The collection of policies, procedures, roles, and responsibilities that direct and support an organization's cybersecurity efforts.

### Objectives
- Define security responsibilities
- Establish security policies
- Manage cybersecurity risks
- Ensure compliance
- Support business goals

---

## Sensitive Personally Identifiable Information (SPII)
A category of Personally Identifiable Information (PII) that requires **stricter protection** because its exposure can cause significant harm to individuals.

### Examples
- Social Security numbers
- Passport numbers
- Driver's license numbers
- Bank account information
- Credit card numbers
- Medical records
- Biometric data

---

# Module 3 Summary

Module 3 introduces the **security frameworks, controls, governance, ethics, and foundational principles** that organizations use to manage cybersecurity risks.

### Key Concepts Learned

- The **CIA Triad** forms the foundation of information security.
- **Security frameworks** provide structured guidance for managing cybersecurity.
- **Security controls** reduce specific risks through administrative, technical, and physical safeguards.
- **Security governance** aligns cybersecurity efforts with organizational goals.
- **Compliance** ensures adherence to laws, regulations, and internal policies.
- **Privacy protection** safeguards personal information from unauthorized use.
- **HIPAA** protects patients' health information in the United States.
- **PHI** and **SPII** require strong security controls due to their sensitive nature.
- **Security ethics** guide professionals in making responsible and lawful decisions.
- The **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)** provides a voluntary, risk-based approach to improving cybersecurity.
