# Terms and Definitions — Course 1, Module 2

## Adversarial Artificial Intelligence (AI)

**Definition:**  
A technique that manipulates **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** and **Machine Learning (ML)** technologies to conduct cyberattacks more efficiently.

**Purpose:**

- Evade security systems.
- Fool AI-based detection models.
- Increase the effectiveness of cyberattacks.

---

## Business Email Compromise (BEC)

**Definition:**  
A type of **phishing attack** in which a threat actor impersonates a trusted individual or organization to gain a financial advantage.

**Examples:**

- Fake CEO requesting an urgent money transfer.
- Fraudulent invoice payment requests.
- Fake vendor payment changes.

---

## CISSP (Certified Information Systems Security Professional)

**Definition:**  
A globally recognized cybersecurity certification awarded by **(ISC)² (International Information System Security Certification Consortium)**.

**CISSP Domains:**

1. Security and Risk Management
2. Asset Security
3. Security Architecture and Engineering
4. Communication and Network Security
5. Identity and Access Management (IAM)
6. Security Assessment and Testing
7. Security Operations
8. Software Development Security

---

## Computer Virus

**Definition:**  
Malicious code designed to interfere with computer operations and damage data or software.

**Characteristics:**

- Requires user interaction to spread.
- Infects legitimate files or programs.
- Can corrupt or delete data.

> **Note:** A **virus** is a type of **malware**.

---

## Cryptographic Attack

**Definition:**  
An attack that targets cryptographic systems or encrypted communications to compromise confidentiality or integrity.

**Examples:**

- Brute-force attacks on encryption keys.
- Cryptanalysis.
- Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) attacks against encrypted communications.

---

## Hacker

**Definition:**  
Any person who uses computers to gain access to computer systems, networks, or data.

**Types of Hackers:**

- White Hat (Ethical Hacker)
- Black Hat (Malicious Hacker)
- Gray Hat (Acts without permission but not always malicious)

---

## Malware

**Definition:**  
Software designed to damage devices, systems, or networks or gain unauthorized access.

**Common Types:**

- Virus
- Worm
- Trojan Horse
- Ransomware
- Spyware
- Adware
- Rootkit

---

## Password Attack

**Definition:**  
An attempt to gain unauthorized access to password-protected systems, devices, or accounts.

**Examples:**

- Brute-force attacks
- Dictionary attacks
- Credential stuffing
- Password spraying

---

## Phishing

**Definition:**  
The use of digital communications to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information or installing malicious software.

**Goals:**

- Steal credentials.
- Deliver malware.
- Obtain financial information.
- Gain unauthorized access.

---

## Physical Attack

**Definition:**  
A security incident that targets both digital systems and the physical environment.

**Examples:**

- Stealing company laptops.
- Damaging servers.
- Unauthorized entry into secure facilities.

---

## Physical Social Engineering

**Definition:**  
An attack in which a threat actor impersonates an employee, customer, or vendor to gain unauthorized physical access.

**Examples:**

- Tailgating
- Wearing fake employee badges
- Pretending to be maintenance personnel

---

## Social Engineering

**Definition:**  
A manipulation technique that exploits **human error** to gain private information, access, or valuables.

**Common Types:**

- Phishing
- Spear phishing
- Vishing
- Smishing
- Baiting
- Pretexting
- Tailgating

---

## Social Media Phishing

**Definition:**  
A phishing attack where the attacker gathers information about a target from social media before launching a personalized attack.

**Purpose:**

- Increase credibility.
- Improve the success rate of phishing attacks.

---

## Spear Phishing

**Definition:**  
A highly targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific person or group, often appearing to come from a trusted source.

**Characteristics:**

- Personalized messages.
- Research-based.
- Higher success rate than general phishing.

---

## Supply-Chain Attack

**Definition:**  
An attack targeting trusted software, hardware, systems, or third-party vendors to introduce malware or exploit vulnerabilities.

**Examples:**

- Compromised software updates.
- Infected third-party libraries.
- Vendor compromise.

---

## USB Baiting

**Definition:**  
A social engineering attack in which a threat actor leaves an infected USB drive where someone is likely to find and use it.

**Goal:**

- Trick users into installing malware.
- Gain unauthorized access to organizational networks.

---

## Virus

**Definition:**  
See **Computer Virus**.

A virus is malicious code that attaches itself to legitimate programs and spreads when executed.

---

## Vishing

**Definition:**  
Voice phishing that uses phone calls or voice communication to trick victims into revealing sensitive information.

**Examples:**

- Fake bank calls.
- Fake IT support.
- Fake government agencies.

---

## Watering Hole Attack

**Definition:**  
A cyberattack where a threat actor compromises a website frequently visited by a targeted group of users.

**Purpose:**

- Infect visitors with malware.
- Steal credentials.
- Gain unauthorized access to target organizations.

---
