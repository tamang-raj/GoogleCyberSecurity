# Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities

## 📖 Overview

This module explains the relationship between **assets, threats, risks, and vulnerabilities**, how they affect an organization's security, and the potential consequences of cyberattacks such as ransomware.

---

# 🏢 Understanding Assets

An **asset** is anything that has value to an organization and needs protection.

## Examples of Assets

- Physical facilities
- Computers and servers
- Customer data (PII)
- Intellectual property
- Business applications
- Networks

---

# ⚠️ Threats

A **threat** is any event or circumstance that has the potential to damage or compromise an organization's assets.

## Examples

- Phishing attacks
- Malware
- Ransomware
- Social engineering
- Insider threats
- Physical theft

---

# 🎯 Risks

A **risk** is the likelihood that a threat will successfully exploit a vulnerability and negatively affect an asset.

## Risk Levels

- 🟢 Low
- 🟡 Medium
- 🔴 High

Risks are evaluated based on their potential impact on:

- Confidentiality
- Integrity
- Availability (CIA Triad)

---

# 🔓 Vulnerabilities

A **vulnerability** is a weakness that attackers can exploit.

## Common Vulnerabilities

- Outdated software
- Weak passwords
- Misconfigured systems
- Human error
- Unpatched applications
- Poor security practices

> **Note:** A risk exists only when both a **threat** and a **vulnerability** are present.

---

# 🛡️ Risk Mitigation

Organizations reduce risks by identifying and fixing vulnerabilities before attackers can exploit them.

## Security Analyst Responsibilities

- Educate employees about phishing
- Promote strong password practices
- Monitor access to critical assets
- Report suspicious activity
- Implement physical security controls
- Reduce organizational vulnerabilities

---

# 💣 Ransomware

**Ransomware** is malware that encrypts an organization's files and demands payment to restore access.

## Impact

- Data becomes inaccessible
- Business operations are interrupted
- Financial losses increase
- Recovery costs can be significant

---

# 🌐 Layers of the Web

## Surface Web

The publicly accessible portion of the internet that can be reached using standard web browsers.

### Examples

- News websites
- Search engines
- Blogs
- Online stores

---

## Deep Web

Content that requires authentication or authorization to access.

### Examples

- Company intranets
- Online banking
- Medical records
- University portals

---

## Dark Web

A hidden part of the internet that requires specialized software (such as Tor) to access.

### Characteristics

- Provides anonymity
- Frequently used for illegal activities
- Can host stolen data, malware, and black-market services

---

# 📉 Impact of Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities

## 💰 Financial Impact

Cyberattacks can result in:

- Business interruptions
- Recovery expenses
- Regulatory fines
- Loss of revenue

---

## 🪪 Identity Theft

Organizations storing sensitive information face the risk of data theft.

Compromised information may include:

- Personally Identifiable Information (PII)
- Financial records
- Login credentials
- Customer information

Stolen data is often sold on the dark web.

---

## 📢 Reputational Damage

Successful cyberattacks can harm an organization's reputation by:

- Losing customer trust
- Generating negative publicity
- Reducing business opportunities
- Increasing legal and compliance issues

---

# 🎯 Key Takeaways

- **Assets** are valuable resources that require protection.
- **Threats** are events that can harm organizational assets.
- **Vulnerabilities** are weaknesses that attackers exploit.
- **Risk** is the likelihood that a threat will exploit a vulnerability.
- Ransomware encrypts data and demands payment for recovery.
- The internet consists of the **Surface Web**, **Deep Web**, and **Dark Web**.
- Cyberattacks can lead to financial loss, identity theft, and reputational damage.
- Security analysts help reduce risks through education, monitoring, and implementing security controls.
